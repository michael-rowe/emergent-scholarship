# Information management for academics

_Building a sustainable system for working with knowledge_

---

## Sit quietly near a little stream and listen

Academic life has become a torrent of information. Every morning brings dozens of emails, journal alerts, social media notifications, RSS feeds, and messages across multiple platforms. You're drowning in potentially valuable sources while simultaneously feeling like you never have enough to say about your research.

The problem isn't lack of access to information—it's that you have no systematic way to work with it. Articles pile up in your downloads folder. PDFs sit unread in your reference manager. Browser tabs multiply. Emails with "interesting" links go unanswered. You save everything, read some of it, and process almost none of it into original insight.

**Most academics confuse information consumption with knowledge work.** Reading articles, highlighting passages, and saving sources feels productive, but these activities don't automatically translate into research outputs, better teaching, or original contributions to your field. That transformation requires a system—a deliberate process for moving information from the fast-moving stream into your own thinking.

This course builds that system, step by systematic step.

## Why academics need information management systems

The volume of information isn't the problem. The quality of information streams varies dramatically, and most academics haven't developed criteria for deciding what deserves attention. Without clear filtering, you end up trying to track everything, which means effectively engaging with nothing.

Even after filtering, information arrives according to its own schedule, not yours. An excellent article lands Tuesday morning when you're preparing lectures. A colleague mentions a relevant book Friday afternoon when you're exhausted. A useful methodology surfaces at 10pm Sunday. Without capture systems, you face impossible choices: disrupt current work, try to remember for later, or let it pass entirely.

Captured sources don't automatically become useful. PDFs in a reference manager, bookmarked articles, saved videos—none of these formats are conducive to the intellectual work academics do. You can't synthesise ideas when they're locked in their original containers. You need extraction processes that transform sources into workable materials.

But extracted notes remain just information—content you've consumed but not transformed. Having more information doesn't lead to better academic work. It often produces the opposite: analysis paralysis, fragmented understanding, and the nagging sense that you're always reading but never producing.

**Information becomes knowledge through processing**—the deliberate work of connecting, elaborating, questioning, and synthesising. This happens through writing, not the formal writing of articles, but the daily practice of working through ideas on the page.

Finally, knowledge work that remains invisible to others creates limited career capital. The distinction between academic work and scholarship lies in sharing: academic work transforms information into understanding for yourself; scholarship shares that understanding to create value for your professional community.

### Lesson 1: Filtering information

- Reviewing captured items weekly to determine what deserves permanent storage
- Building a curated personal library of high-value sources
- Solving the temporal mismatch between when information arrives and when you can engage with it

### Lesson 3: Extracting information

Information arrives in formats you can't easily work with—PDFs, videos, podcasts, emails, images. This lesson establishes extraction practices:

- Transforming captured sources into workable materials
- Engaging actively through highlighting and annotation
- Creating literature notes associated with original sources
- Moving information from locked formats into systems you can actually use

**Practical outcome:** You'll convert raw sources into annotated materials you can synthesise, search, and build upon—the foundation for original thinking.

### Lesson 4: Processing information

Extracted notes are just materials. This lesson transforms information into knowledge through deliberate processing:

- Adding your own thinking to create something that didn't exist before
- Finding connections, elaborating concepts, questioning arguments
- Synthesising across sources to develop original insights
- Establishing daily writing habits that generate genuine understanding

**Practical outcome:** You'll develop the craft of knowledge work—your particular way of thinking about problems, connecting ideas, and generating understanding that distinguishes your contribution from anyone else's.

### Lesson 5: Sharing information

Processing creates value for your own work, but sharing creates scholarly contribution. This lesson establishes sustainable sharing practices:

- Transforming private work into shareable formats
- Adding commentary and synthesis rather than just passing along sources
- Building career capital through visible intellectual contribution
- Integrating sharing into existing workflows rather than treating it as separate work

**Practical outcome:** You'll contribute processed insights back to your professional community, building reputation and creating opportunities while making your intellectual work visible.

# Lesson 2: Capturing

**Solution-Problem Discussion**: The lesson discusses various note-taking apps and their features, which addresses the problem of capturing information from diverse sources effectively. However, it might be beneficial to also discuss strategies for quickly assessing the value of information before capturing it.

A practical strategy for quickly assessing the value of information before capturing it involves a three-step process:

1. **Relevance Check**: Determine if the information aligns with your current research, teaching, or professional development needs. If it doesn't directly contribute to your ongoing projects or goals, it might not be worth capturing.
2. **Source Evaluation**: Quickly assess the credibility of the source. Prioritize information from reputable academic journals, books, or recognized experts in the field. Be cautious with unverified online sources or opinions.
3. **Quick Skim for Key Insights**: Before deciding to save the information, skim it for key insights or conclusions. Look for summaries, abstracts, or bullet points that encapsulate the core message. If these elements provide value or new insights, it's a signal that the full content may be worth capturing.

1. **Introduction**: Present a research article relevant to the participants' field.
2. **Identification of Key Sections**: Guide participants to identify abstract, introduction, methodology, results, and conclusion sections.
3. **Highlighting Key Information**: Instruct participants to highlight key statements, findings, and arguments in each section.
4. **Summarization Exercise**: Ask participants to write a concise summary of each section, focusing on the essence of the content.
5. **Comparison and Discussion**: Have participants share their summaries in small groups, discussing the similarities and differences in what they identified as key information.
6. **Reflection**: Conclude with a reflection on the process, discussing how this exercise can be applied to their regular academic work.
